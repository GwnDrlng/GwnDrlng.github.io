Product Management Leader. My last decade runs through B2B SaaS in cybersecurity, digital forensics, and fintech, with P&L ownership and a habit of doing what I say I will do.

How I got here

My first product work was a digital pivot at a tea company, reporting directly to the CEO. We implemented Salesforce Marketing Cloud and rebuilt how the company reached its customers. Email platform costs dropped 60%, sales grew 323%, online orders grew 314%, and email marketing reach doubled. The lesson stuck. The most leverage in a business sits at the intersection of customer understanding and operational systems.

Manulife came next, and it changed my trajectory twice. The work taught me enterprise product at scale, with five dispersed delivery teams, regulatory constraints, SAFe, and stakeholders from front-line advisors to the C-suite. The PMP taught me something else. After passing a four-hour exam like that, I decided I could do university, and with my kids old enough to be self-sufficient I enrolled part-time at Wilfrid Laurier. Economics with Financial Management was a deliberate choice. Reaching executive roles would take real finance skills, and I looked around and found no mentor or leader who was going to teach me. So I went and built them myself.

Magnet Forensics followed, where the customers were law enforcement agencies investigating serious crimes and I became a named inventor on a patent-pending algorithm. Then Arctic Wolf, where the business line I built from zero was validated by IDC within two years. The domains change. The work stays consistent. Understand the problem before designing the solution, make the call clearly, and stay close to the customer and the data in equal measure.

How I work

The financial and business case is mine to own, and I mean that literally. Companies expect PMs to carry business acumen and rarely see it practiced. I build the pricing models, defend them to Finance, and co-own the P&L that results.

My engineering partnerships run deep. Customer insights go from my interviews directly to the engineering team, and the goal is a team that owns the build, planning the stories and dependencies themselves while I hold the strategy, the market, and the higher-altitude accountabilities. At Magnet, I built a severity matrix for forensic artifacts, mapping what action each artifact type warranted, so the team could make those calls without me. The engineering manager I built it with watched his team take real ownership, and that trust is the foundation the partnership runs on.

Decisions come fast, and that is a practiced skill. A fast decision is one made with enough information to be directionally correct, paired with the willingness to update when new data arrives. Waiting for certainty is usually a way to avoid making a call.

High-stakes domains draw me in because the feedback is clearer. When a forensic investigator runs your triage tool in the field, or a company activates an IR retainer mid-breach, you know fast whether the product held up. And through all of it, I connect with people as people. Colleagues get respect and get known, and that is why they trust me with hard things.
