Running a Product Practice on AI

My product practice runs on agentic systems I design and build myself. The same systems ran inside Arctic Wolf's product organization, where I led AI adoption for PMs and designers, and several now live in the open on GitHub where anyone can read the architecture. AI belongs in my work for the same reason it belongs in a product. It closes a measurable gap and stays bounded by what a person can stand behind.

The operating stack

Four capabilities carry most of my product work.

Claude Code CLI and Copilot in VS Code

This is the primary workflow for competitive analysis, PRD drafting, research synthesis, and documentation. At Arctic Wolf I built and ran a competitive analysis agent producing structured intelligence, positioning gaps, GTM overlaps, and response vectors, in a format that went directly into strategic planning decks and exec briefings. It compressed hours of research cycle time into minutes for consumption, validation, and human synthesis.

See a public system in the same pattern, daily-sentiment-analysis on GitHub →

LLM-as-judge discovery workflows

AI runs through my discovery process end to end. Discussion guides get pressure-tested before fieldwork, question sets build from the tested plan, responses get evaluated against clear rubrics with evals and LLM-as-judge scoring, and transcripts get normalized for structured analysis. Discovery becomes repeatable instead of dependent on memory and note quality.

See the public version of this system, research-engine on GitHub →

Template-driven PRD workflows

Opinionated PRD templates with built-in prompts force the hard questions early, from job-to-be-done and customer segment through success metric, known unknowns, and build-vs-buy framing. First-draft time dropped by roughly 60 to 70 percent, and the early-stage thinking improved because the hard questions became visible before anyone started writing solutions.

M365 Copilot as the accessible baseline

Meeting summaries, communication drafts, and research starting points. The goal was making AI the default starting point for documentation and communication across the whole team, power users and everyone else alike.

Enabling an organization, treated as a product problem

The behaviorally hardest part was enabling PMs and designers, people with no prior reason to open a code editor, onto VS Code, Claude Code, and Copilot. The program treated adoption as a product problem with a value proposition, a planned learning curve, and friction removed before the change was asked for. That meant clear onboarding, relevant use cases, a quick win in the first session, and a follow-up loop to catch where people got stuck.

The biggest barrier to internal AI adoption is giving people a reason to change how they work when what they currently do works fine. "Here's what AI can do" is a demo. "Here's how you would have solved that problem you mentioned last week, in under five minutes" is adoption.

The teams that got most productive had the fewest training hours. They had a clear first use case, reached a working result fast, and built habit from there. That is activation design, and it works the same way internally as it does for customers.

What I learned

Internal tooling deserves the same product rigor as customer-facing work. Value proposition, activation design, instrumentation, iteration. Skipping any of them shows up the same way it does with customers: quiet abandonment.

Building these systems myself changed how I lead AI product work. The roadmap conversations, the eval discipline, and the build-vs-buy calls all get sharper when the PM in the room has shipped agents with their own hands.
