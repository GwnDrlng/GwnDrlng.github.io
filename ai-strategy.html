AI Adoption at Arctic Wolf: Shipping It, Not Just Talking About It

Most "AI strategy" in product organizations is a slide deck. This one was a working program — from a competitive analysis agent to VS Code enablement for designers — built and run by me while simultaneously building Incident360.

Company Arctic Wolf Networks
Role Sr. PM — AI Tooling Lead (Internal)
Timeline Jan 2024 – May 2026
Domain AI Strategy · Internal Tooling · Team Enablement

The problem with "AI strategy"

By 2024, every product organization was claiming an AI strategy. Most of them meant: "we are evaluating Copilot licenses" or "we have a slide in the all-hands about how we're approaching AI." Very few had a working program with real tooling, real enablement, and real velocity outcomes.

At Arctic Wolf, I had two mandates at once: build Incident360 as a product, and lead internal AI adoption for the product and design organization. The second one wasn't in the job description. It emerged from the first — because I was already building with AI tooling myself, and the team around me was watching what was possible.

The tooling stack

The internal program ran on three tools:

Claude Code CLI

I used Claude Code CLI as my primary AI-augmented workflow for product work — competitive analysis, PRD drafting, research synthesis, documentation generation. The key discovery was that the value wasn't in any single output. It was in compressing the time between "question" and "informed decision" — getting from a new competitive signal to a structured analysis in minutes instead of hours.

M365 Copilot

M365 Copilot was the accessible on-ramp for non-technical teammates. I led enablement sessions that moved people from "I've heard of this" to "I use this in every meeting summary and every first draft." The goal wasn't to turn everyone into a power user — it was to establish a working baseline where AI was the default starting point for documentation, communication, and research tasks.

GitHub Copilot

GitHub Copilot was deployed to PMs and designers alongside VS Code enablement. This required an upfront investment in teaching people who had never opened a code editor why VS Code was useful for them. Once past that, the combination of Copilot + structured markdown tooling changed how PRDs, specs, and research documents were written and maintained — treating product documentation like code, with version history and structured format.

What I shipped

Competitive analysis agent

I built a competitive analysis agent that could be run against a competitor briefing or market signal and produce a structured output: positioning gaps, feature comparisons, GTM overlaps, and suggested response vectors. The output wasn't a summary — it was actionable intelligence in a format that could go directly into a strategic planning deck or exec briefing.

For a cybersecurity company operating in a fast-moving market, competitive lag is expensive. This agent compressed competitive response time significantly — moving from a week-long research cycle to a same-day structured brief.

Automated PRD and documentation workflows

I built template-driven PRD and documentation workflows that gave the product team a structured starting point for any new initiative. The key design decision was to make the templates opinionated — not blank starting points, but structured documents with built-in prompts for the questions a PRD should answer: the job-to-be-done, the customer segment, the success metric, the known unknowns, and the build-vs-buy decision framing.

This reduced the time to first draft on major product documents by approximately 60-70%, and more importantly, improved the quality of early-stage thinking by making the hard questions visible before anyone started writing solutions.

PM and designer enablement on VS Code

The most behaviorally complex part of the program was enabling PMs and designers — people who had no prior reason to open a code editor — to work in VS Code. I ran a structured enablement program that treated this as a product adoption problem: clear onboarding, relevant use cases, quick wins in the first session, and a follow-up loop to capture where people got stuck.

What I learned

The biggest barrier to internal AI adoption isn't access to tools. It's giving people a reason to change how they work when what they're currently doing is working fine.

The only enablement sessions that moved the needle were the ones that started with a problem someone actually had. "Here's what AI can do" is a demo. "Here's how you would have solved that problem you mentioned last week, using this tool, in under five minutes" is adoption.

The second learning: internal tooling deserves the same product rigor as customer-facing work. The teams who were most productive after the program weren't the ones who got the most training hours — they were the ones who had a clear first use case, got to a working result fast, and built habit from there. That's activation design. It works the same way internally as it does for customers.
