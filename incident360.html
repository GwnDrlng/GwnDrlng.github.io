Turning Incident Response Into a Recurring Product Line

Arctic Wolf had a transactional incident response offering that customers bought after a breach. I replaced it with a tiered retainer family designed to be activated before one ever happens — and shipped it without a dedicated dev team for 18 months.

Company Arctic Wolf Networks
Role Senior Product Manager, IR · P&L Co-owner
Timeline Jan 2024 – May 2026
Domain Cybersecurity · SaaS · B2B

The bet

The cybersecurity market was selling incident response as a break-glass service: a company gets breached, they call someone, a team shows up. The model was reactive by design, which meant every engagement started from zero — no relationship, no pre-loaded context, no trust built before the crisis.

The hypothesis was that if you could shift a customer's relationship with IR from transactional to retainer — a standing commitment with pre-built runbooks, readiness touchpoints, and a proactive services track — you'd change the economics for both sides. Customers would be better prepared. Arctic Wolf would have predictable, recurring revenue in a market that had none.

That bet was non-obvious for one reason: the market didn't exist yet. Nobody had productized IR at scale. The question wasn't "can we build it?" — it was "will customers pay for it before they need it?"

The constraint

The hardest part wasn't the product. It was building it without dedicated engineering capacity.

For the first 18 months, Incident360 was developed entirely through borrowed capacity — teams with competing roadmap priorities, no dedicated sprint, no dedicated PM-Engineering pod. That meant three things had to be true for the product to ship:

  • Ruthless sequencing. Every decision to build something meant a decision to not build something else. I established governance frameworks that gave cross-functional teams a shared decision-making language, which turned into faster yes/no cycles and fewer priority wars.
  • Internal customers first. The IR Services team was the product's first internal customer. I applied the same research, scoping, and instrumentation rigor to their operational workflows as to customer-facing surfaces. Trust from that team was the reason I could keep borrowing capacity.
  • Short feedback loops. I structured individual interviews across the first 20 Tabletop Exercise deliveries and CSAT collection on all services closes — turning live service delivery into a continuous product research channel.

What I built

The Incident360 family launched as three tiers: JumpStart, Incident360, and Incident360 Plus — designed to serve different customer profiles along a readiness spectrum, from "we know we need IR coverage" to "we want full retainer depth with proactive services and threat intel."

The portal experience was designed to work on two timelines at once:

  • Pre-breach: IR Plan completion, readiness touchpoints, proactive service scheduling, and threat intel digests all visible from a single pane. Customers could see their own preparedness gap.
  • In-breach: A single "Report a Breach" escalation button surfaced at the top of the IR dashboard — zero friction from portal to response team engagement.

Activation architecture

One of the most important discoveries came from comparing bundled vs. standalone retainer customers early in the launch. The two cohorts had divergent activation patterns — standalone customers were not following the same onboarding path as bundled ones, and the gap was showing up in engagement metrics.

I tracked both cohorts in Tableau, then designed and shipped a standalone-specific onboarding track: rebuilt welcome email sequence, added a portal orientation call covering emergency and proactive services, and integrated with a concurrent marketing campaign to maintain a consistent first impression across every channel.

Longer term, I defined activation milestones across the full Incident360 lifecycle (first portal login, contacts added, IR Plan completion, proactive service request submitted) and surfaced all signals in Planhat with triggered lifecycle messaging — giving CS, Sales, and IR Services direct access to product engagement data within their own workflows, with no routing through product.

Go-to-market: MSP, reseller, and insurance

Incident360 wasn't designed to sell only direct. I built the MSP, reseller, and insurance carrier go-to-market tracks — including international expansion with regulatory market fit work — which extended the product's total addressable market beyond what a direct-only motion could reach.

What I learned

The biggest insight from Incident360 wasn't a product insight — it was an organizational one. Building cross-functional trust is a prerequisite for building good product without headcount. The 18 months without a dedicated dev team worked because the IR Services team, engineering leads, and GTM teams all had skin in the outcome. They weren't helping because I asked them to — they were helping because they believed in where it was going.

The second thing: activation is not the same as onboarding. Getting customers into the product is a logistics problem. Getting them to experience value before they need it is a product problem. Those require completely different instrumentation, different messaging, and different ownership across CS and product.
