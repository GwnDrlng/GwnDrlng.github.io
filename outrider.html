Weeks to Minutes, and Knowing When to Stop

Magnet Outrider compressed forensic evidence discovery from weeks to minutes for the investigators who needed it most. The work included launching customer-requested ML models in the AXIOM suite, inventing a patent-pending detection algorithm with two engineering partners, and then making the call to fold Outrider into every AXIOM license when the standalone market told us what it could pay for.

Company Magnet Forensics
Role Senior Product Manager
Timeline Mar 2021 – Jan 2024
Domain Digital Forensics · B2G · AI/ML

The problem

Before this work, scanning a seized device meant keywords and curated hash lists, on desktop devices only. A device went back to the lab, acquisition started, and an investigator waited days or weeks for results. Seize too many devices and the lab backlog grows. Seize too few, or the wrong ones, and the evidence that identifies a victim may never be found.

Magnet's customer base was approximately 80% law enforcement and federal agencies, including the FBI, DEA, and HSI. In their cases the cost of lag is measured in people. Every day of evidence delay is a day a victim stays unidentified.

Triage was the answer, a fast-path tool an investigator could run on-scene, showing the signal before full acquisition completed. The open question was whether it could be accurate enough to trust in production.

What launched in AXIOM

The AI/ML capabilities I productized shipped inside the AXIOM suite, and every one of them started as a customer request. The portfolio included classification models for hand recognition, gang signs, animals, and nudity detection built on skin-tone analysis for CSAM cases, alongside a CSAM classification model quantized to run on CPU so it worked on the hardware investigators had in the field. These models were available to law enforcement customers only.

My role was the connective tissue between the investigator and the model. I worked closely with engineering so the team understood the customer need behind each request, evaluated on-prem and cloud AI/ML capabilities for investigator workflows, and made the productization calls about what a repeatable, defensible product could support.

How I judge an ML claim

Forensics raised the bar on ML evaluation, and it shaped a discipline I still hold teams to. Every model score needs a baseline, a held-out test set, and an expression of uncertainty before it counts as evidence. Precision and recall pull against each other, and the threshold between them is a product judgment about which error the investigator can better afford. In a domain where a model's decision may be defended under challenge in court, an interpretable model can beat a marginally more accurate black box.

The hackathon, the patent, and finding victims faster

Outrider launched iPhone triage scanning, extending fast evidence discovery to mobile devices. Then a hackathon changed the ceiling of what triage could do. A large cross-functional group shipped a small ML model, and alongside it three of us created a patentable algorithm, myself and two engineers.

The algorithm, P1280USP, detects origin material in forensic investigations. Beyond flagging that known CSAM exists on a device through hash matching against victim identification databases like VICS and CAID, it can indicate when material is first-generation, meaning it was created on that device. That distinction is the difference between possession and active abuse, and it moves victim identification from days, weeks, or never down to hours.

My contribution as named inventor came from knowing how forensic investigators work their cases. I brought the investigative methodology, and my two engineering partners proved the technology could support it, verifying that the data our assumptions relied on existed and was dependable.

The algorithm used hashing and matching techniques against VICS and CAID databases to identify CSAM origin material during triage, reducing forensic evidence discovery from weeks to minutes for investigators worldwide.

Forensic Cross-Case Analysis

Beyond single-device triage, Forensic Cross-Case Analysis shipped as a capability that let investigators connect material across multiple cases and datasets. Organized crime and trafficking networks span many investigations, and linking material across cases without manual correlation was a meaningful operational upgrade.

The call I made

Outrider proved the triage concept. It shipped, it worked, and investigators used it. The structural problem was the buyer, and law enforcement budgets are a chain. The investigator who needs the tool, the purchaser who approves it, and the person who eventually signs the check are three different people with three different incentives. Free tools covered enough of the same ground to stall a new line item, and at $1,000 per dongle Outrider was never a large one to begin with.

Thoma Bravo's acquisition sharpened the question. The push was to cut costs and concentrate value in the core product, AXIOM. The decision was to add Outrider to every AXIOM license, which meant fewer line items on the bill, an easier purchase to approve, and immediate reach to every AXIOM customer without a new buying decision.

Folding Outrider into AXIOM extended the technology's reach further than the standalone model ever could have. The fit existed. The standalone SKU was the wrong container for it.

What I learned

The Outrider arc is the clearest evidence I can offer when someone asks about product judgment. Plenty of PMs can launch. Fewer can make a credible case that the right move is to stop scaling something and integrate it instead.

The lesson is that market existence and market willingness to pay are different questions. Investigators needed Outrider's capabilities, which adoption and the algorithm's validation made clear. "The market needs this" and "the market will pay for this as a standalone SKU in this procurement environment" are separate hypotheses, and conflating them is how teams burn resources defending a go-to-market the market has already voted against.

The kill decision is the hardest PM decision. It is also the one that most clearly reveals judgment over process.
