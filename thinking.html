Frameworks

How I think

Five frameworks I've developed from real product decisions — not theory, not best practice borrowed from a blog. Each one is grounded in something that happened.

01

What you don't build matters more than what you do

The kill decision is the hardest thing a PM does — and the most revealing. Anyone can find a reason to build something. The judgment is in finding a reason to stop.

At Magnet Forensics, Outrider was a technically validated product with a real problem space. The patent-pending algorithm worked. Investigators used it. But the standalone go-to-market was fighting against free government tools and procurement cycles that weren't structured to buy a new SKU. The right call was to fold the capabilities into the AXIOM license family — expanding reach to every existing customer, without requiring a new purchase decision.

The framework: When evaluating whether to keep scaling a product, separate three questions that often get conflated. Does the market have the problem? (Yes, usually.) Does the market acknowledge the problem? (Sometimes.) Will the market pay for your specific solution, in this form, at this price point, through this channel, in this procurement environment? That last question is where the real work is — and it's the one teams skip when they're attached to what they've built.

02

Activation and onboarding are not the same thing

Onboarding is a logistics problem: get the user into the product, set up their account, show them the basics. Activation is a product problem: did the user experience the value they came for before they decided whether to care?

With Incident360, I discovered this the hard way. Bundled customers — who came to IR through existing Arctic Wolf relationships — activated differently than standalone buyers. Standalone customers were going through the same welcome flow and then going quiet. The onboarding worked fine. The activation didn't.

The framework: Define your activation milestone before you write your onboarding spec. What is the specific moment when a user has experienced enough value to form a habit? (For Incident360: first IR Plan completion and first Readiness Touchpoint requested.) Then work backwards from that moment to design onboarding that accelerates it — not onboarding that ticks administrative boxes and hopes activation happens on its own.

The second implication: you need separate instrumentation for onboarding completion and activation rate. They're different metrics. If you're only measuring one, you're missing half the story.

03

AI in product: earn it or it earns you

There are two kinds of AI features in B2B SaaS right now. The first kind closes a real gap — a workflow the user has, a task they're spending time on, a decision they're making with worse information than they should have. The second kind exists because someone decided the product needed to say it uses AI.

At Magnet Forensics, AI/ML in the forensic product earned its place because the user's actual problem — surface the relevant material in a 500GB device image before the investigation window closes — was genuinely unsolvable without it. The algorithm didn't add AI to an existing workflow; it created a workflow that couldn't exist otherwise.

The framework: Before adding any AI capability, write down the answer to: what does this user currently do instead? If the answer is "nothing — they just don't have this information/capability at all," that's a real AI use case. If the answer is "they do it manually in two minutes," you probably don't need AI. You need better UX.

The discipline is rejecting the reverse question: "where in our product could we add AI?" That question will always find an answer. It won't always find a good one.

04

In regulated markets, compliance is table stakes — trust is the product

Regulated markets — financial services, cybersecurity, law enforcement — have a baseline that most teams treat as the goal. Meet the compliance requirement, ship the feature, move on. That's the table stakes. The product work is everything above that line.

At Arctic Wolf, I embedded NIST SP 800-61, NIST SP 800-84, HIPAA, and Cyber Essentials Plus not as certification checkboxes but as customer-facing capabilities inside Incident360. A customer using Incident360 Plus wasn't just paying for IR retainer coverage — they were getting a product that helped them assess and improve their own regulatory posture. The compliance framework became a product feature, not a legal requirement.

At Manulife, AODA compliance was non-negotiable. But the investment in accessibility design — real hypothesis testing, not just screen reader compatibility — produced a better enrolment experience for every user, not just users with disabilities. Constrained design produces better design.

The framework: Map every regulatory requirement to a user outcome, not just a compliance checklist item. Ask: if a user experienced this requirement as a product feature, what would they get? That reframe consistently surfaces product improvements that would have been invisible if you were only thinking about compliance.

05

B2G is a different motion — and most product people don't know it

Selling to governments — or building products that government agencies buy — has a set of constraints that B2B product instincts work against. Government procurement doesn't run on a sales cycle. It runs on a budget cycle, an approval chain, and a risk model that values continuity over improvement.

The Magnet Forensics customer base was approximately 80% law enforcement and three-letter agencies. That context shaped everything: how features were prioritized (auditability, chain of custody, not UX delight), how pricing was structured (agency-wide licensing, not per-seat), how adoption happened (mandated rollout, not viral growth), and how feedback was collected (formal channels, not Slack).

The framework: Before building for a government buyer, answer three questions that don't apply in commercial B2B. First: is this in an existing budget line, or does someone have to create a new one? New budget lines in government take 12-18 months minimum. Second: who holds approval authority, and how many people are in that chain? Third: is there a free tool that does 70% of this — because if there is, the government will use it, regardless of how much better yours is.

Understanding these constraints is what let me make the call on Outrider with confidence. The technology was solid. The buyer motion was the problem.
