Frameworks

How I think

Five frameworks developed from hard product decisions. Each one is grounded in something that happened, and each one links to the case study where it played out. I hold frameworks loosely. The goal and the mission decide, and the framework serves the outcome.

01

What you don't build matters more than what you do

The kill decision is the hardest thing a PM does, and the most revealing. Anyone can find a reason to build something. The judgment is in finding a reason to stop.

At Magnet Forensics, Outrider was a technically validated product solving an acute problem. The patent-pending algorithm worked. Investigators used it. The standalone go-to-market was fighting free government tools and procurement cycles that had no structure for buying a new SKU. The right call was folding the capabilities into the AXIOM license family, which expanded reach to every existing customer without requiring a new purchase decision.

The framework. When evaluating whether to keep scaling a product, separate three questions that often get conflated. Does the market have the problem? (Yes, usually.) Does the market acknowledge the problem? (Sometimes.) Will the market pay for your specific solution, in this form, at this price point, through this channel, in this procurement environment? That last question is where the hard work sits, and it is the one teams skip when they are attached to what they have built.

02

Activation and onboarding are different problems

Onboarding is a logistics problem. Get the user into the product, set up their account, show them the basics. Activation is a product problem. Did the user experience the value they came for before they decided whether to care?

Incident360 taught me this the hard way. Bundled customers, who came to IR through existing Arctic Wolf relationships, activated differently than standalone buyers. Standalone customers went through the same welcome flow and then went quiet. The onboarding worked fine. The activation didn't.

The framework. Define your activation milestone before you write your onboarding spec. What is the specific moment when a user has experienced enough value to form a habit? (For Incident360 that was first IR Plan completion and first readiness touchpoint requested.) Then work backwards from that moment to design onboarding that accelerates it, instead of onboarding that ticks administrative boxes and hopes activation happens on its own.

The second implication is that onboarding completion and activation rate need separate instrumentation. They are different metrics, and measuring only one hides half the story.

03

AI in product: earn it or it earns you

There are two kinds of AI features in B2B SaaS right now. The first kind closes an existing gap, a workflow the user has, a task eating their time, or a decision they are making with worse information than they should have. The second kind exists because someone decided the product needed to say it uses AI.

At Magnet Forensics, AI/ML belonged in the forensic product because the user's problem, finding the relevant material in a 500GB device image before the investigation window closes, was unsolvable without it. The algorithm created a workflow that could not exist otherwise.

The framework. Before adding any AI capability, write down the answer to one question. What does this user currently do instead? If the answer is "nothing, they simply lack this capability," that is a strong AI use case. If the answer is "they do it manually in two minutes," you probably need better UX instead of AI.

The discipline is rejecting the reverse question, "where in our product could we add AI?" That question will always find an answer. It will rarely find a good one.

04

In regulated markets, compliance is table stakes and trust is the product

Regulated markets, financial services, cybersecurity, and law enforcement among them, have a baseline that most teams treat as the goal. Meet the compliance requirement, ship the feature, move on. That is the table stakes. The product work is everything above that line.

At Arctic Wolf, NIST SP 800-61, NIST SP 800-84, HIPAA, and Cyber Essentials Plus became customer-facing capabilities inside Incident360 rather than certification checkboxes. A customer using Incident360 Plus was getting a product that helped them assess and improve their own regulatory posture on top of the retainer coverage. The compliance framework became a product feature.

At Manulife, AODA compliance was non-negotiable. The investment in accessibility design, with hypothesis testing that went beyond screen reader compatibility, produced a better enrolment experience for every user. Constrained design produces better design.

The framework. Map every regulatory requirement to a user outcome rather than a compliance checklist item. Ask what a user would get if they experienced this requirement as a product feature. That reframe consistently reveals product improvements that stay invisible when compliance is the only lens.

05

B2G is a different motion, and most product people don't know it

Government procurement runs on a budget cycle, an approval chain, and a risk model that values continuity over improvement. B2B product instincts work against every one of those, and building products government agencies buy means relearning the motion.

The Magnet Forensics customer base was approximately 80% law enforcement and three-letter agencies. That context shaped everything. Feature priority went to auditability and chain of custody ahead of UX delight, pricing structured around agency-wide licensing instead of per-seat, adoption arrived through mandated rollout rather than viral growth, and feedback came through formal channels.

The framework. Before building for a government buyer, answer three questions that have no commercial B2B equivalent. First, is this in an existing budget line, or does someone have to create a new one? New budget lines in government take 12 to 18 months minimum. Second, who holds approval authority, and how many people are in that chain? Third, is there a free tool that does 70% of this? If there is, the government will use it, regardless of how much better yours is.

Understanding these constraints is what let me make the Outrider call with confidence. The technology was solid. The buyer motion was the problem.
